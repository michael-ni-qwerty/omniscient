# What Is Omniscient?

*A plain-language guide — no technical background needed.*

---

## In one sentence

**Omniscient is a place where you can bet on whether something in the real world will happen — and an AI, not a company, decides who was right.**

Examples of the kinds of questions you could trade on:

- "Will Bitcoin be above $100,000 on December 31st?"
- "Will it rain in London on the day of the marathon?"
- "Will this movie make more than $500 million at the box office?"
- "Will Team A win the championship?"

You put money behind your opinion. If you're right, you get paid. If you're wrong, the people who were right share your money.

---

## Why does this need to exist?

People already love predicting things — sports, elections, crypto prices, the weather. There are even existing apps for it (the most famous is called Polymarket). But today's versions have **four big frustrations**:

### 1. Very few questions to bet on
Existing platforms only offer a small, hand-picked list of questions, because a **human team** has to manually create and manage each one. Want to bet on something they didn't think of? Too bad.

**Omniscient's answer:** anyone can create a market on *any* question that can be answered with facts. The menu is effectively infinite.

### 2. It takes forever to find out who won
On older platforms, **a human referee** decides the outcome — and that can take days or even weeks. You're left waiting to get paid.

**Omniscient's answer:** an AI reads the real-world evidence and decides the outcome in seconds to minutes.

### 3. You have to trust a company with your money
On a normal betting site, you hand your money to the company and *hope* they pay you back and don't cheat. If they go bust or run off, your money is gone.

**Omniscient's answer:** your money is held by transparent, automated code (a "smart contract") — **not** by a company. Nobody, including us, can secretly take it or fake a trade in your name. The rules are public and enforced automatically.

### 4. The referee could cheat
If one company both runs the bets *and* decides the winners, they have an obvious temptation to cheat.

**Omniscient's answer:** before the AI reveals its decision, it publicly "locks in" what it's about to say (think of it as sealing its answer in a glass envelope that everyone can see is sealed). This makes it impossible to quietly change the answer afterward to favor one side.

---

## How it works — the simple version

Think of it in four steps:

### Step 1 — A question is created
Someone posts a question with a clear deadline and clear rules for what counts as "yes" or "no." To stop people from spamming junk questions, creating a market requires putting down a small refundable deposit.

### Step 2 — People trade
You buy a "Yes" or "No" position depending on what you believe.

- The **price** of a "Yes" share tells you the crowd's estimate of the odds. A "Yes" share priced at 70 cents roughly means "the crowd thinks there's a 70% chance."
- A complete winning share is always worth **$1** when the question resolves. So if you bought "Yes" at 70 cents and it turns out to be true, you collect $1 — a 30-cent profit per share.
- You can buy and sell anytime before the deadline, just like a stock.

All of this trading happens **instantly** behind the scenes, so prices feel fast and live, like a real exchange.

### Step 3 — The question gets resolved
When the deadline arrives:

- **For price questions** (like "Will Bitcoin be above $100k?"), the system reads a trusted, tamper-resistant price feed and answers automatically. No opinion needed — it's just a fact.
- **For real-world questions** (like "Did this event happen?"), an **AI** reads the evidence, cites its sources, and proposes an answer. It publicly locks in its answer *before* revealing it, so it can't be secretly altered.

### Step 4 — You get paid
Once the answer is final, the winners automatically split the pot. The money moves directly to the winners through the automated code — no waiting on a company to process a withdrawal.

## "Is my money safe?" — the honest answer

This is the most important question, so here's a straight answer.

**What protects you:**

- **You stay in control.** Your funds sit in open, automated code — not in a company bank account. We cannot spend your money or invent fake trades, because every trade must be signed and approved by *you*.
- **Everything is public.** The rules, the trades, and the AI's locked-in answers are all recorded openly and can be checked by anyone.
- **Stable money.** Balances are held in **USDC**, a digital dollar — so $1 stays roughly $1, and you're not exposed to wild crypto swings just by holding a balance.

**What you should understand about the risks:**

- **You can lose your bet.** If you're wrong, you lose the money you put on that position. That's how the game works.
- **The AI is not perfect.** AIs can occasionally get facts wrong. That's why every AI decision is backed up by published evidence and a window of time for people to challenge a bad answer before money moves.
- **This is early-stage software.** Like anything new, it should be treated with appropriate caution.

> ⚠️ **Important:** Prediction markets may be regulated or restricted where you live. Availability depends on your location and local laws.

## The big idea, restated

Today, predicting the future is slow, limited to a few topics, controlled by companies that hold your money, and judged by referees you have to trust.

**Omniscient flips all of that:** open to any factual question, judged quickly by AI with public evidence, with your money under your own control the entire time.
